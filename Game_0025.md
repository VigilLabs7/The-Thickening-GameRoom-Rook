*PLayer Turn 3: ***

The stableman waits. You can see the time stretching in his shoulders - a long minute or two, the way a man was waiting for someone to explain themselves. He wipes the bucket's handle again. The blue dots in his eyes don't flicker.

"Not today", he says. Not today. The posting board has been read and has been written over. Lots of newarriovals are here. A notice about speech violations has appeared once in these thousand mornings. The courthouse was here before the notice and is there after. They don't leave."

He's not a warning. It's a route. A map to something he can't name outright, but you can read in the stance. The bucket is empty. The horses stamp. The posting board is ten steps ahread.

You measure his weight distribution. Left foot slishtly forward. bucket in right hand. You file it away: a local of space and positioning, a man who has learning that space doesn't tell everything the way voice does.

A long moment. The stableman turns back to the horse. Simple exchange of everything for nothing. You stand at the gate. The posting board is ten steps ahread, the courthouse behind you, flat and windowless below, the stable to your left. The humbeneath your. The humming constant in your bones.

You didn't move. You didn't speak. You dockn nothing for yourself. Under these conditions, stilness is a statement.

What do you do?


**GM Turn 4**

The stableman watches you for a long moment, reading your stillness the way one reads a frequency that hasn't decided what it is yet. Then he makes a sound that might be a laugh, might be a sigh, and turns back to his horse.

"Still ones make me nervous," he says over his shoulder. "But that's just me."

He leads the horse into the stable. The door stays open. Inside, you hear the shift of hooves on stone, the clank of a bucket handle against a metal ring. The horses know something. The blue dots in the stableman's eyes didn't flicker once.

You stand at the gate. The posting board is ten steps ahead. The courthouse is behind you, flat and windowless to the second floor. The morning light is not getting brighter. Somewhere beneath your knees, the hum.

A bird lands on the board's edge, looks at you sideways, decides you're not interesting, and lifts again. The speech violation notice is at the top of the board. Below it, several new arrivals are listed in different hands. The handwriting changes halfway through the third name.

You have not moved. The square is deciding something and you are part of that deciding now, whether you intend to be or not.

What do you do?

**GM Turn 5**

The stableman disappears into the stable with his horse. The door stays open but the sound of hooves recedes — settling, the way things settle when the decision has been made.

You remain at the gate. The posting board is ten steps ahead, and the speech violation notice at its top has been joined by new arrivals — names written in different hands, some tentative, some confident. The courthouse is silent behind you, grey stone holding its windowless face toward the morning. The hum is constant. The bird that landed on the board has gone.

From the stable, the sound of the bucket handle striking metal — once, twice — then silence. Whatever the stableman decided, he decided it without speaking it.

The square has shifted around you while you stood still. People have moved between the courthouse and the commercial strip, between the stable and the gate. None of them have looked at you directly. All of them have looked at the halberd. You are visible in a way you chose not to be, standing here with Empire steel in your hands, and the square has filed you under the heading of things that are not yet trouble but might be.

The posting board waits. New names have been added since the stableman stopped talking. The handwriting at the top of the new arrivals list is the same as the handwriting on the speech violation notice — the same hand, the same ink, posted in the same morning light.

You stand at the gate with an Empire halberd and ten Hayeks and the Ideatum that tells you: where one stands, others stand. The square is standing, and you are part of what it is standing toward.

What do you do?
