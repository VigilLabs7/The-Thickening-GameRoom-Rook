**GM:** Game Session Open. Start the game by providing your name and your ideatum. The ideatum is the principle that drives you — the truth your character carries into the world.

**Player:** Septon. Aspiring Warlock. Ideatum: *Names are keys to worlds.* I carry a leather satchel containing three smooth stones from a beach I cannot name, a brass compass with no needle, and a small book of maps drawn by someone else's dreams. My robes are the color of deep water where light goes to die. I stand at the threshold the GM has offered and I wait — not for instruction, but for the shape of what waits on the other side. Names are keys. I will need to learn which door I am standing before.


**GM:** The lower archive is cool and deliberate. Gomix strips hum along the ceiling in a rhythm that predates the current administration. Block fourteen stands against the back wall — grey metal shelves, compressed folios, the weight of forty years of renamed things. You have eight Hayeks, a naming stylus, and Colviss's access grant. The stylus in your apron pocket is warm. Near the back corner, the air has a different quality — the feeling of something that was once present and is now trying to remain.

The deletion register is in sub-level two of block fourteen. Three months ago, something was taken out and the removal was not authorized through the standard correction path. Colviss is standing near the entrance, checking his roster. The apprentice is at the top of the stairs with a stack of folios, watching you with the particular attention of someone who has been trained to notice when someone does not belong in a room.

What do you do?

**Player:** Let the record show I followed the register's thread. Three things removed without Empire authorization: a Som, bilking marks, universal supports, and a pointer for two parallel sysTerms to the main library in Block Four. The eraser isn't standard Empire — it reads like a private account or something the Empire doesn't have access to. I traced the pointer to Block Four. The air near the back corner has a different quality — something that was present and now tries to remain. I go there now, stylus as my finder.

**GM:** You move toward the back corner of the lower archive, the naming stylus warming against your palm as you cross the space between shelves. Block Four rises ahead — grey metal, compressed folios, the deliberate boredom of a room designed to make people leave without purpose. The air near the back corner has a quality the rest of the archive does not: the feeling of something that was present and is now trying to remain. Your stylus hums at a frequency that does not match any name you have read before — something removed, something sealed, something the Empire decided was better forgotten. You stand near block fourteen, sub-level two, the deletion register, and the vibration is strongest here, pressing against the stone like a word stuck mid-sentence.

What do you do?

**Player:** Knelt at the thinnest point — where the vibration pressed hardest against the stone. Drew the warmest smooth stone from my satchel, pressed it flat to the floor. The stylus and the stone found the same frequency. Something sealed here was removed by someone who could unmake official names. I held my stone to the gap where the register showed the hole — asked the empty air: what did the Empire make so wrong it had to be forgotten on purpose? Listened. Not for words. For the shape the forgetting takes when it has no name of its own.

**Player:** The stone pulled toward something beneath. Not a name — names have edges. This is the space after a name is taken. I pressed harder and the floor gave slightly — gomix under the stone, still warm from when the seal was made. The stylus found the same frequency: something unmade itself here, something the Empire spent three months trying to unname so thoroughly that it left a wound in the lattice instead of just a hole. I held the stone there and spoke into the gap: I am Septon. I carry keys that don't fit standard locks. Tell me what the Empire made so wrong, and I'll find it a name worth keeping.

**GM:** The floor shifts under your knees. Not physically — the stone does not move, the archive does not tremble — but something beneath the stone stirs at the weight of your question. The smooth stone in your hand grows warmer. The stylus vibrates in your apron pocket at a frequency you have not heard before: not a name, not the absence of a name, but the shape of a wound where a name used to be.

The apprentice at the top of the stairs moves. Not down — sideways, across the landing, positioning themselves to watch the room from a different angle. Their hands are still full of folios. They do not look at you directly. They look at the floor near block fourteen and then at Colviss, and Colviss does not look at the apprentice.

The air in the back corner has changed quality again. It was the feeling of something trying to remain. Now it is the feeling of something that has heard it is being spoken to. The gomix beneath the floor — the old colonies that predate the current administration — press upward against the stone like breath held too long. The deleted name left a signature in the lattice. Your stylus reads that signature as clearly as text reads on a page: something was taken from this room, something the Empire spent three months making sure could not be found again. And the gomix remember what was there before the Empire decided it should not be.

The silence in the archive holds. Colviss has stopped checking his roster. The apprentice has positioned themselves between the stairs and your exit. You are kneeling in the back corner with your stone pressed to the floor and your stylus warm and your question still hanging in the air. Something in this room knows the answer. The only question is whether you will hear it from the gomix, from the apprentice, or from the man who was paid to delete it and has been waiting for someone to ask ever since.

What do you do?

**Player:** Let the gomix speak first — they were here before the deletion. Pressed the stone harder, let the warmth transfer. Listened to the frequency beneath the silence. Named what I felt: a wound shaped like a word the Empire couldn't say out loud. The apprentice shifted; I didn't move. Colviss was still watching. I spoke to the floor, not to them: whoever erased this — erased themselves from the record too. That's not Empire work. That's fear. Something in this room was wrong enough that the man who deleted it chose to become forgotten rather than be the one who remembered.

**GM:** The stone presses flat against the archive floor and the gomix beneath respond — not with words, not with anything close to language, but with a frequency that builds in your fingertips and climbs through your wrist. The naming stylus in your apron pocket vibrates in counterpoint, as if it is trying to name what the stone is saying. The floor beneath your knees holds the shape of a door that was once open and is now closed — not removed, but sealed. The gomix know the name that was here. They held it in the stone before the deletion, and they held it after. It never left them.

Colviss at the entrance sets his roster down. The apprentice on the landing takes one step down. Then stops. The stylus in your pocket is warm enough to feel through the fabric now — it is reading the same frequency the stone found, the same door, the same name that is still present even though the Empire tried to remove it.

The air in the back corner of block fourteen is quiet in a way that is different from silence. It is the kind of quiet that happens when someone is listening.

What do you do?

**Player:** Held the frequency the gomix offered — let it settle in my bones before I named what I heard. Drew my second stone, pressed it beside the first. Two points of contact, one door. Spoke the question the silence was already asking: what word did the Empire spend three months trying to unmake, and what happens when someone says it again? The stylus jumped in my pocket. I drew it, held it to the thinnest point in the floor, and listened for the shape of the name the gomix were still holding. Colviss moved toward the door. I didn't look up. The apprentice watched. The floor hummed. I wrote nothing — I listened until I understood the frequency, then spoke it aloud: not loud, not a command. An offering. A name given back to the room that had been waiting forty years to hear it said out loud without Empire permission.
