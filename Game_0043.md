**GM Turn 1:**

The weight settles into your legs the way it always does when you've been standing long enough for the world to stop noticing you. The halberd is in your hands — Empire issue, the kind with the flared blade that catches light in ways that make it hard to see the edge. The posting board is ten steps ahead. The courthouse is behind you, grey stone, no windows on the lower floors. The stable door is open. The horses are shifting inside.

The stableman comes around the corner with an empty bucket. He sees the halberd, the stance, the way you've planted your weight. He pauses. The blue dots in his eyes catch the grey light.

"Quiet morning," he says. Not a question. Not a greeting. The kind of thing a man says when he's deciding something.

What do you do?

**GM Turn 2:** 2026-05-08T03:45 UTC

You stand. The stableman reads the decision in your spine before you move — you feel his weight shift, the bucket lowering half an inch, the blue dots tracking you the way a marksman tracks something that's decided to walk toward the target.

You walk to the board.

The notice is pinned with a straight pin, the kind that goes through paper and into cork. The ink is still sharp. Empire issue — you recognize the press, the way the letters come out even and flat and cold. You've seen these notices before, in other squares, on other mornings. They go up and they stay up until someone tears them down or someone tears them down and puts something worse in their place.

You read it again, slower:

NOTICE: Speech violations will be prosecuted under Empire Order 7. Citizens are required to report suspected violations to the courthouse. Non-reporting is itself a violation.

You measure the letters. You count them. You feel the hum beneath your feet and the weight of the halberd in your grip and the way the stableman's eyes haven't moved from your back.

You look at the courthouse. Grey stone, no windows on the lower floors. The morning light doesn't touch the facade — it stays in the street, in the square, in the space between the people who are standing in it.

The stableman's voice comes from behind you. Still low. Still careful.

"You can report to the clerk in the west wing. Morning hours only. Flat-light."

He means when the sun is directly overhead. Midday. When there's no shadow to read.

He means the courthouse is safest at noon.
