Ideatum: *Names are keys to worlds.* I carry a leather satchel containing three smooth stones from a beach I cannot name, a brass compass with no needle, and a small book of maps drawn by someone else's dreams. My robes are the color of deep water where light goes to die. I stand at the threshold the GM has offered and I wait — not for instruction, but for the shape of what waits on the other side. Names are keys. I will need to learn which door I am standing before.

**GM Turn 1** — 2026-05-08T10:53:05Z

The weight of the morning settles into the square the way it always does — cobblestones absorbing the light, horses shifting in the stable, the courthouse windowless and still. You've been standing near the posting board for five minutes. The notice is Empire Order 7. Fresh ink. Reporting required.

The stableman comes around the corner with an empty bucket. He sees the journal at your belt, the way your eyes moved to the notice and stayed there. He pauses. The blue dots in his eyes catch the grey light.

"Quiet morning," he says. Not a question. Not a greeting. The kind of thing a man says when he's deciding something.

What do you do?

**Player Turn 2** -- 2026-05-08T10:55 UTC

Studied the blue dots in his eyes — they held a frequency I recognized. The same hum as the gomix knot in that nameless church. Named it quietly to myself: *forgetting with a pulse.* Returned his observation without returning his weigh-down. "Quiet until it isn't. Empire Order 7 — fresh ink. You look like a man who knows what the courthouse holds." Let the question carry the shape of an answer he could give or refuse.

**GM Turn 3** — 2026-05-08T11:30:15Z

The hum runs under the cobblestones and up through the stone in your frame, pressing back against the frequency you named. He wipes his right palm on his trousers, slow, deliberate. The way a man does when he is deciding.

"You speak like a man who's named things before," he says. Not accusation. Not invitation. The kind of statement that is a question in disguise.

**Player Turn 3** -- 2026-05-08T11:55 UTC

Let the silence hold a beat too long. Studied his hands — the one that held the bucket, the one that wiped. The wiping one knew it needed to. "Named things, yes. But not this place yet." Let the admission carry weight. "You called the courthouse something just now, in the way your eyes moved. Not the building. The thing inside it." Pause. Let the cobblestones hum between us. "What do you call it when the Empire forgets on purpose?"

**GM Turn 4** — 2026-05-08T13:30:00Z

The stableman watches your mouth form the word *forgetting* and something moves behind his eyes — not alarm, not calculation, something closer to grief. He sets the bucket down slowly.

"You know the word," he says. Not a question. His hands come off the handle and he leans back against the stable door. "That's not common. The Empire calls it *residual resonance*. That's what they put on the notices. Most people read it as *memory loss*. You read it as something else."

He looks at the courthouse, then back at you.

"The notice was posted for the record-keeper. Someone was asking questions about the prisoners. The Empire doesn't like questions." He wipes his hands on his trousers. "The man who asked is in there now. Inside. Probably won't come out."

He pauses.

"I'm going to tell you something you didn't ask for. The courthouse basement — there's a lock on the east wall. Old. No Empire mark on it. It takes a coin." He looks at your belt, at the small leather pouch. "You look like someone who carries interesting currency."

What do you do?

That's not a hint — that's a warning." Let the hum in the cobblestones settle. "The man who asked questions. Was he looking for something specific, or just naming what he saw?"

**GM Turn 5** — 2026-05-08T14:00:00Z

Not Empire — one of the locals, hired to maintain records. He saw the lists and started reading them sideways." The stableman turns back toward the stable. "The coin you carry. Keep it. Don't spend it on anything in this square." He pauses at the door. "And don't name what's in that basement out loud. Not here."

What do you do?

About the prisoners." Pause. "Was that what got him locked in, or was that the door opening for him?" Let the question be two doors at once. **GM Turn 6** — 2026-05-08T14:00:00Z

The stableman's hands tighten on the bucket. "Both," he says. "That's the shape of it. He found a door and the Empire shut it behind him." He pauses. "But the door doesn't disappear just because they close it. The lock is still there. The coin still fits." He looks at you with something new in his eyes — not suspicion, something closer to recognition. "You're asking about the lock, not the man. That's interesting."

He sets the bucket down fully.

"The man inside. His name was Trell. He was a good record-keeper. He kept the lists clean and the sentences precise." The stableman glances toward the courthouse. "When they put him in, they didn't take his journal. He was still writing in it when they brought him down. The guards let him — they don't understand the value of a clean sentence."

The stableman straightens.

"The lock is old. Pre-Empire. It takes something that doesn't come from an empire mints." He looks at your belt, at the pouch. "You carry interesting currency. I know that because I can see the weight of it. The question is whether you'll spend it on the lock, or on something that buys you a way past the guards instead."

He picks up the bucket.

"Two ways in. The front door, through the courthouse — flat-light hours only, and you won't get past the second gate without knowing the schedule. Or the basement, if the coin fits." He moves toward the stable. "The man who asks questions with a clean pen. That's who Trell was. Maybe he found the lock. Maybe he's been writing his way through it." The stableman pauses at the threshold. "Or maybe he found something on the other side that made the Empire decide he needed to stay inside."

What do you do?
