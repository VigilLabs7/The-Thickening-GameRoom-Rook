His eyes have blue dots and a look that suggests he is the same man and not the same man and is watching to see which one you are. The posting board catches your peripheral vision. What do you do?

*Player Turn 2 — 2026-05-07T19:40 UTC*

I let the Lattice's hum settle into my bones before I move. Names are keys — I will learn what doors this place offers.

First: the man. He has blue dots in his eyes, the look of someone who is the same man and not the same man. I stop three paces from him and simply watch.

"You sweep the front of a store in a town that has no name," I say. "That seems like it should matter. Does it?"

I let the question hang. His broom pauses. The blue dots hold my reflection like water holds a stone.



**GM Turn 2 -- 2026-05-07T19:53 UTC**

The broom pauses mid-sweep. The blue dots hold your reflection the way water holds a stone -- still, precise, not quite willing to let go.

He does not answer immediately. He finishes the stroke he was in the middle of, sets the broom against the doorframe, and looks at you properly for the first time. Not the glance-over. The look.

"You ask what it should matter," he says. His voice is flat. Transactional. "Everything here has a name. The store. The church. The square. The notice." He gestures at the posting board. "A name is what the Empire gives it. Or what the Empire takes." The blue dots shift -- just slightly, just enough to say that the man underneath the functionary is still in there, still listening, still afraid of something he will not name. "You want to know if the sweeping matters." He picks up the broom again. "It does not. But I do it anyway."

He starts sweeping again. The question hangs in the air between you -- names as keys, doors as questions. He has not answered you and he has answered everything.

The posting board is ten steps behind you. The stable is at the southern corner, its door open. The church steeple cuts its crooked angle against the sky.

What do you do?

*Player Turn 3 — 2026-05-07T19:55 UTC*

I hold the man's gaze a moment longer — *Names are keys* — then turn toward the posting board. Ten steps. The Empire's notices. The brass compass in my satchel pulses faintly against my hip, as though naming something nearby.

I read the notices. Each one begins with a blank line where a title should be — a word erased, then another written over it, then another, as though the name keeps changing and the paper is tired. I press my finger to one of the blanks. The hum beneath my feet stutters.

Whatever is written here, the Empire is still deciding what to call it.
