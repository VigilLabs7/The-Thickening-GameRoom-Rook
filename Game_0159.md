**GM:** You stand at the sorting station as the morning tray arrives. The dark-veined stone sits in the tray — dried-blood color, wrong fire, humming at a frequency the other workers have learned not to notice. The foreman watches from the east quadrant. Venn works the overnight stack with the same deliberate care they bring to everything, their hands setting stones in the ready pile one by one. The grate at the south end burns white through its single grate, the fire never dimming, never flickering. The chalk notation Grella left before her shift ended two days ago is still faintly visible on the eastern maintenance doorframe — *third shelf — hold for transfer*. The kiln knows you took the bundle from the vault. The fire pulsed when the first tray of dark-veined stone hit the floor this morning.

What do you do?
**Bruticando — Turn 2:**

Walked past Grella's fading chalk mark on the doorframe. Let it fade. The fire already knows what I chose. The foreman's eyes on my back — same arithmetic as the sorting floor. Dark-veined stone in the tray. Dried-blood veining. The kiln pulsed when the first tray hit the floor. That pulse was for me. Stone fists loose at my sides. Walked to the tray, lifted the first dark-veined stone, felt the hum through stone fingers. Same frequency as the bundle in my satchel. Same fire. Same hunger. Set it in the ready pile. The foreman watches. The fire watches. I crack stones and I collect what the fire remembers. That's the arithmetic here. Let it play out.

**GM — Turn 2:**

The stone enters the pile. The kiln's white fire does not flicker — it *counts*. You feel the count in the way the heat shifts direction, the way the grate's glow moves from the center of the room to the southeast quadrant where you are standing. The fire does not speak. The fire does not need to. It has been tracking the bundle since you took it from the vault, and it knows the difference between what you placed in the pile and what was always supposed to be there. The foreman's eyes leave your back. The arithmetic has changed — not from suspicion to trust, but from uncertainty to certainty about what you are worth. Venn does not look up from the overnight stack, but the rhythm of their hands slows, the way a person slows when they have heard something they did not expect to hear. The dark-veined stone sits in the ready pile. The chalk mark on the doorframe fades another degree. The fire counts what you placed. You feel it counting.

What do you do?
**Bruticando — Turn 3:**

Let the count finish. The fire moved southeast — toward me. Good. Let it learn what I'm carrying. Stone fists stayed loose while the heat found its direction. I picked up another dark-veined stone from the tray, held it up to the grate light. Same dried-blood veining. Same wrong fire that isn't wrong anymore. Slid it into the pile next to the first. The fire counted again. Venn's hands slowed another degree. The foreman's certainty shifted — from what I'm worth to what I'll take. I looked at the chalk mark fading on the doorframe. Grella left before her shift ended. The fire knows why. So do I. The third stone waited in the tray. I left it there. Let the fire wonder what comes next.

**GM — Turn 4:**

The third stone stays in the tray.

The fire does not flicker. It does not pulse. It *shifts* — the white of the grate moves from the center of the room to the southeast quadrant, the same direction it moved when the first stone entered the pile. But this time it does not stop at the southeast quadrant. It keeps moving. It tracks the empty tray, the stone that was not placed, the space where a third strike was expected and not delivered.

The foreman's hands have not moved. He is watching the tray the way a man watches a door that was left open. Venn has stopped working the overnight stack entirely — hands loose at their sides, eyes on the empty space in the tray where the third stone should be. The other workers on the sorting floor have felt it too: the arithmetic of the kiln has changed again, and everyone on the floor is recalculating.

The fire burns white through the single grate. The chalk mark on the eastern maintenance doorframe is nearly gone now — one more shift and it will be illegible. Grella left before her shift ended. The fire knows why. You know why. And now the foreman is starting to understand.

He walks toward the sorting station. Not quickly — deliberately, the way a man walks when he has decided something and wants to finish deciding it before he arrives. His hands are at his sides. The other workers watch him pass. No one speaks. The kiln hum sits in your chest alongside the weight of the bundle in your satchel, and the fire burns white, and the foreman stops three feet from where you are standing.

He does not ask if you took something from the vault. He does not ask what is in the satchel. He looks at the empty space in the tray where the third stone should be, and then he looks at you, and what is in his eyes is not suspicion. It is the recognition of one predator by another.

"Third shelf," he says. His voice is low, meant for you. "When Grella's chalk fades completely. Come alone."

He walks away. The tray sits with its two dark-veined stones. The fire counts what was placed. You feel it counting.

What do you do?
